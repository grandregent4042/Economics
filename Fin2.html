① What is a Derivative?
Definition

📄 Derivative = Value from Underlying

"A contract whose value depends on an underlying asset — not intrinsically valuable itself"
Underlying Assets:
📈 Stock 📊 Index (Nifty) 🥇 Commodity (Gold) 💱 Currency (USD/INR) 💹 Interest Rate
3 Core Uses

🎯 Why Derivatives Are Used

🛡️
Hedging
Reduce / transfer risk
📈
Speculation
Profit from price change
⚖️
Arbitrage
Profit from price difference across markets
⚠️ Economic Survey Warning
Too much derivatives trading → "Financialization of Savings" → Diverts money from productive sectors

⑤ Farmer Example — Very Important for Prelims
Scenario 1 — No Hedge

😟 Without Protection

Farmer plants wheat
→
Market prices fall at harvest
→
Farmer LOSES income
❌ Full exposure to price risk
Scenario 2 — Futures Hedge

✅ With Futures

Farmer locks price via futures
→
Price falls at harvest
→
Gains from futures offset spot loss
✅ Price risk transferred
Scenario 3 — Put Option

🛡️ With Put Option (Insurance)

Farmer buys Put Option (pays premium)
→
Price ↓ → Exercise option → Protected
→
Price ↑ → Ignore option → Profit from market
✅ Acts like crop insurance

📌 Futures vs Options: Key Prelims Distinction Futures = Obligation | Options = Right Forwards = OTC = Default Risk
🔥
Final Connection — The Core Idea
Derivative = Risk management tool whose value depends on underlying asset
Futures = Compulsory, standardized exchange-traded contract
Options = Insurance-like contract — buyer has right, not obligation
Commodity Exchange = Platform for price discovery (MCX = metals, NCDEX = agri)
📄 Contract = Value from Underlying 🛡️ Hedge = Risk Transfer ⚖️ Futures ≠ Options 🌾 NCDEX = Farmer's Friend 🥇 MCX = Metals/Energy ⚖️ Regulator = SEBI
