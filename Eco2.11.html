③ RWA, CRAR & Capital — The Core Mechanics
Risk Weighted Assets (RWA)

⚖️ Not All Loans Are Equal

Loan TypeRisk WeightExample
Govt. Securities0%Risk-free
Mortgage / Collateral50%Home loan
Personal / Unsecured100%Personal loan
RWA = Loan × Risk Weight
Higher risk → Higher RWA → More capital needed
CRAR Formula

📐 Capital to Risk Weighted Asset Ratio

CRAR = Capital ÷ RWA
Expressed as percentage
Why CRAR Matters
Higher CRAR = More cushion to absorb losses
Bank 1 CRAR 40% vs Bank 2 CRAR 30% → Bank 1 is safer
Higher CRAR = Higher depositor safety 🔥
India vs Global Requirement

🇮🇳 RBI's Stricter Norms

Tier 1 Capital (Equity + Retained Earnings) 7%
Tier 2 Capital (Bonds + Subord. Debt) 2%
Capital Conservation Buffer (CCB) 2.5%
India Total CRAR (RBI Requirement) 11.5%
Global minimum = 10.5% | India = 11.5% (RBI stricter)

⑤ NPA Impact & Bank Recapitalisation
NPA Spiral

⚠️ When Loans Become NPAs

1️⃣ Loan becomes NPA (Non-Performing Asset)
2️⃣ Bank must make provisions (set aside money)
3️⃣ Capital reduces → CRAR falls below minimum
4️⃣ Bank cannot lend → Credit crunch in economy
5️⃣ Slower economic growth 📉

The Core Connecting Idea
Banks take deposits → give loans → face credit risk. To prevent collapse, Basel Norms require minimum capital buffers (CRAR). India follows Basel III via RBI at 11.5% CRAR. When capital falls dangerously, PCA activates. When banks become too large to fail, they become D-SIBs with extra safeguards. All roads lead to: protect depositors → maintain financial stability → sustain credit growth.
